Background: Pseudocholinesterase Deficiency

Definition and Pathophysiology

Definition:

Rare genetic or acquired deficiency of butyrylcholinesterase enzyme that metabolizes succinylcholine and mivacurium, leading to prolonged neuromuscular blockade

Epidemiology:

  • Incidence: 1 in 3,200 to 1 in 5,000
  • Higher in Persian Jewish, Alaskan Native, European descent
  • Autosomal recessive inheritance

Affected Drugs:

  • Succinylcholine (most significant)
  • Mivacurium
  • Ester local anesthetics (procaine, tetracaine)
[תמונה 12: דיאגרמה של האנזים]

Sources: Trujillo & West, StatPearls 2023; Medscape 2024

Genetic Variants

Types and Clinical Significance

Neuromuscular Junction Physiology

Acetylcholine and Succinylcholine Mechanism

[תמונה 15: דיאגרמה של הצומת הנוירו-שרירי]

Normal Acetylcholine (ACh) Function:

  • ACh released from motor neuron terminal
  • Binds to nicotinic receptors on muscle endplate
  • Causes depolarization → muscle contraction
  • Rapidly broken down by acetylcholinesterase at the junction
  • Allows repolarization and next contraction cycle

Succinylcholine Mechanism:

  • Structure: Two ACh molecules linked together
  • Binds to same nicotinic receptors as ACh
  • NOT broken down by acetylcholinesterase
  • Causes prolonged depolarization
  • Phase I: Initial depolarization → fasciculations
  • Phase II: Sustained depolarization → flaccid paralysis

Succinylcholine Metabolism:

  • 90-95% metabolized by pseudocholinesterase in plasma before reaching junction
  • Only 5-10% reaches neuromuscular junction
  • Diffuses away from junction → metabolized by plasma pseudocholinesterase
  • Normal duration: 5-10 minutes

Why No Fasciculations? The First Critical Clue

Understanding the Pathophysiology

[תמונה 16: השוואה ויזואלית]

Normal Succinylcholine Action:

  • 90% of the drug is hydrolyzed by plasma pseudocholinesterase before it reaches the muscle.
  • The remaining 10% reaches the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), causing initial, uncoordinated muscle firing.
  • This initial firing is visible as fasciculations, followed by paralysis.

Action with PChE Deficiency:

  • The enzyme is deficient, so the drug is not hydrolyzed in the plasma.
  • A much larger dose (effectively a 10x overdose) floods the NMJ all at once.
  • This high concentration causes a rapid, profound depolarizing block that is too intense to produce fasciculations, leading directly to flaccid paralysis.

Clinical Takeaway:

The absence of fasciculations is not a sign of drug failure. It is the earliest and most specific sign that a patient cannot metabolize succinylcholine, predicting a significantly prolonged duration of paralysis.

Sources: Trujillo & West, StatPearls 2023; Cornelius & Jacobs, Anesth Prog 2020; OpenAnesthesia 2024

Pregnancy and Pseudocholinesterase

Physiological Considerations

Pregnancy Effects on PChE Activity:

  • Decreased activity by 20-30% during pregnancy
  • Lowest levels at term and early postpartum
  • May unmask heterozygous variants
  • Returns to normal 6-8 weeks postpartum

Mechanism:

  • Increased plasma volume (hemodilution)
  • Decreased hepatic synthesis
  • Hormonal factors (estrogen, progesterone)
[תמונה 17: אישה בהריון בייעוץ רפואי]

Relevance to our case:

Patient at 15+6 weeks gestation - pregnancy likely unmasked mild deficiency

Literature Review: Core Anesthesia Recommendations

Orphananesthesia Guidelines (Feb 2025)

[תמונה 18: ספרות רפואית]

Contraindicated Drugs: Absolute Avoidance

  • Succinylcholine & Mivacurium: These are absolutely contraindicated in known or suspected PChE deficiency.
  • Ester Local Anesthetics: Drugs like tetracaine and procaine will have a prolonged duration and increased risk of systemic toxicity. Amide local anesthetics (e.g., Lidocaine, Ropivacaine) are safe.

Safe Alternatives & Airway Management

  • For Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI): Use Rocuronium at 0.9 - 1.2 mg/kg instead of succinylcholine.
  • General Relaxant Choice: Non-depolarizing agents that are not metabolized by PChE (e.g., Rocuronium, Vecuronium, Cisatracurium) are the agents of choice.
  • Reversal: The availability of Sugammadex for rocuronium makes it a particularly attractive and safe alternative.

Source: Schulze-Berge J, Hüppe T. Anaesthesia recommendations for Pseudocholinesterase Deficiency. Orphananesthesia. 2025.

Literature Review: The Risk of Anesthesia Awareness

Orphananesthesia Guidelines (Feb 2025)

[תמונה 19: מטופל מודאג]

Alarming Data from the Danish Research Unit:

Registry data from patients with confirmed PChE deficiency revealed a significant gap between knowledge and practice:

  • 72% of patients experienced "premature awakening" (i.e., anesthesia was stopped while they were still paralyzed).
  • Only 40% of patients underwent any neuromuscular monitoring at all.
  • Of those, only 20% were monitored at the end of the procedure, which is the most critical time to assess for recovery.

The Clinical and Medicolegal Consequences

This clinical gap leads to a high risk of conscious paralysis, which is associated with intense distress and a significantly increased risk of developing Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD). The guidelines emphasize that failure to monitor is a major medicolegal pitfall.

Source: Schulze-Berge J, Hüppe T. Anaesthesia recommendations for Pseudocholinesterase Deficiency. Orphananesthesia. 2025.

Literature Review: Diagnosis & Treatment Strategy

Orphananesthesia Guidelines (Feb 2025)

The Diagnostic Pathway

Since routine screening is not standard, diagnosis follows a path of suspicion:

  1. Clinical Event: Patient fails to recover from succinylcholine/mivacurium.
  2. Functional Testing: Confirm with laboratory tests:
    • PChE plasma activity level.
    • Dibucaine Number: Measures enzyme quality. (40-70% suggests heterozygote, <20% suggests homozygote).
  3. Genetic Confirmation: PCR testing can determine the specific hereditary cause.
[תמונה 20: מעבדה עם בדיקות דם]

The FFP / Transfusion Controversy

While transfusing Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP) or whole blood *can* provide exogenous PChE to shorten recovery time, the guidelines state this is "generally deemed unnecessary."

Reasoning: The prognosis with supportive care (continued ventilation and sedation) is excellent, and the additional risks and costs associated with blood products are not justified.

Source: Schulze-Berge J, Hüppe T. Anaesthesia recommendations for Pseudocholinesterase Deficiency. Orphananesthesia. 2025.

Literature Review: A Landmark Systematic Review (Part 1)

Andersson et al., Anaesthesia (2019)

[תמונה 21: מחקר מדעי גדול]

Key Quantitative Findings: Prevalence & Duration

This major systematic review analyzed 131 publications to consolidate the clinical data on Butyrylcholinesterase (BChE) deficiency. The findings provide a clear picture of risk:

  • Prevalence: The review confirmed the genetic prevalence:
    • Heterozygotes: Affects approximately 1 in 25 to 1 in 50 people.
    • Homozygotes: Affects approximately 1 in 2,000 to 1 in 5,000 people.
  • Duration of Apnea after Succinylcholine: The data showed a distinct dose-response based on genotype.
    • Heterozygotes: Experienced a mildly prolonged block, typically lasting 9-13 minutes (a 1.5 to 2-fold increase).
    • Homozygotes: Experienced a severely prolonged block, with apnea lasting from 60 minutes to several hours.

Clinical Context for Our Case:

The duration of apnea in our patient (~27 minutes until extubation, with residual weakness) is significantly longer than the average for a heterozygote, but much shorter than a typical homozygote. This highlights that pregnancy (an acquired cause) combined with a heterozygous genotype can result in a clinically severe, intermediate phenotype.

Source: Andersson, M.L., Møller, A.M. and Wildgaard, K. (2019), Butyrylcholinesterase deficiency and its clinical importance in anaesthesia: a systematic review. Anaesthesia, 74: 518-528.

Literature Review: A Landmark Systematic Review (Part 2)

Andersson et al., Anaesthesia (2019)

The Problem of Mismanagement & Awareness

The review's most powerful conclusion was not just about the condition itself, but about how it is frequently mismanaged, leading to preventable harm.

  • Failure to Diagnose: In many reviewed cases, prolonged apnea was incorrectly attributed to other causes, leading to inappropriate treatments (like reversal agents) that could worsen the block.
  • Anesthesia Awareness: The most severe complication highlighted was the risk of the patient waking up while still fully paralyzed, an experience the authors note can be "devastating."
[תמונה 22: ביטוי דאגה של מטופל]
"The consequences of mismanagement, including awareness with recall of paralysis, can be devastating for the patient and may lead to complaints and litigation."

Core Recommendations from the Review:

Based on the evidence, the authors strongly advocate for a change in standard practice:

  • Routine Neuromuscular Monitoring: They argue that because the condition is often unknown preoperatively, quantitative neuromuscular monitoring should be a standard of care whenever succinylcholine or mivacurium is used.
  • Patient Education and Family Screening: Once a diagnosis is made, it is imperative to counsel the patient, provide a medical alert card, and strongly advise family members to undergo testing.

Source: Andersson, M.L., Møller, A.M. and Wildgaard, K. (2019), Butyrylcholinesterase deficiency and its clinical importance in anaesthesia: a systematic review. Anaesthesia, 74: 518-528.

Literature Review: The Patient Experience of Awareness

Davis et al., Int J Anesthetics and Anesthesiology (2021)

Case Data: Two Heterozygous Patients

This paper presents two compelling cases of undiagnosed heterozygous PChE deficiency. Notably, both patients had a non-contributory family history.

  • Case 1 (40yo female): Total recovery time was 210 minutes. Her dibucaine number was 34, confirming heterozygosity.
  • Case 2 (38yo female): Total recovery time was 360 minutes (6 hours). Her dibucaine number was 44, also confirming heterozygosity.
[תמונה 23: מטופל במצב של awareness]

Patient Perspectives on Anesthesia Awareness:

"I felt trapped within my body. I could hear people speaking to me, but I couldn't respond. I couldn't open my mouth or move my arms or legs... I was terrified during the incident."
"I felt an enormous amount of panic and doom... It felt like I was locked into place. It was a very disturbing and surreal feeling... One year later and I can still vividly remember the sensation."

Key Discussion Points & Clinical Implications:

  • The Trauma of Awareness: The authors powerfully highlight that prolonged paralysis can be "quite distressing" and that it is "imperative that patients are re-sedated in a timely manner" to limit this psychological trauma.
  • Contraindication of Reversal Agents: The paper explicitly advises against using agents like neostigmine, stating that an "attempted reversal with these agents will prolong the neuromuscular blockade."
  • Importance of Patient Education: Both patients expressed relief at receiving follow-up and education, underscoring the need for clear counseling and family testing after a diagnosis.

Source: Davis, J., Vincent, A., & Shanmugam, G. (2021). Pseudocholinesterase Deficiency and Patient Perspectives. International Journal of Anesthetics and Anesthesiology, 8(124).

Take Home Messages

For the Practicing Anesthesiologist

[תמונה 24: אנסתזיולוג בכנס]

Trust the Silence. No fasciculations after succinylcholine is not a benign event or drug failure. It is your first, most reliable sign of PChE deficiency. Your diagnostic process begins at that moment.

Pregnancy Unmasks the Condition. The physiological drop in PChE during pregnancy acts as a "stress test." A negative family history in a pregnant patient is not reassuring and should not lower your index of suspicion.

Paralyzed is Not Asleep. If you suspect prolonged paralysis, your immediate priority after securing the airway is ensuring adequate sedation. Prevent the trauma of anesthesia awareness at all costs.

Extubate Based on Data, Not Gestalt. Clinical signs of recovery are notoriously unreliable. Safe extubation is only guaranteed with objective evidence—do not remove the tube until you have a confirmed quantitative TOF ratio > 0.9.
